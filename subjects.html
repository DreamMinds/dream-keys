The following records document the participants who contributed to the Dream Keys study across Cohorts A and K, 1966–1970. All identifying information has been removed and replaced with the subject codes used in the study's internal records. Dream journal excerpts are reproduced with consent obtained at debrief. Records are maintained in accordance with Article 4 of the Institute's Internal Ethics Guidelines and are not to be shared with any party outside the Institute without written authorisation from the principal investigator or the Robinson Estate.

Subject A-3
Cohort: A  ·  Active: February–October 1966
Lead researcher: D.U.U. Robinson
Age (at enrolment)34
OccupationCivil engineer
Total sessions18
Outcome — Full match0
Outcome — Partial match2 (position)
Outcome — Near match4
Study completionFull

A-3 was among the most conscientious participants in the first cohort. He kept unusually detailed dream journals — typically three to five pages per session — and was noted for his consistent use of spatial and structural language in describing his dreams: he described environments in terms of dimensions, distances, and materials, in a way the team attributed to his professional background. This consistency made his journals easier to score reliably than those of many other subjects.

Recurring themes in A-3's dream content included water, staircases, and fish — all appearing with a frequency that the team noted but did not attempt to explain within the framework of the study. In two sessions, he reported specific integers in spatial contexts: "climbing to the twenty-second floor of a building I knew had fifty-two floors" (Session 7, card position 22) and "counting eleven pillars and knowing there were two more I couldn't see" (Session 14, card position 14). Both were scored as partial position matches.

Dream journal extract — Session 7, 22 April 1966
"I was in a building I understood to be very tall, though I couldn't see the top from where I stood. I climbed the stairs — there were no lifts — and I knew, in the way you know things in dreams, that I was on my way to the twenty-second floor. Not the twenty-first, not the twenty-third. I didn't question it. When I arrived, the floor was filled with shallow water, and there were large shapes moving beneath it that I didn't look at directly."

A-3 completed all eighteen sessions and participated in the final debrief. When the hypothesis was explained to him, he was quiet for a long moment before saying: "That's a strange thing to find out about yourself." He did not elaborate, and the team did not press him.

Study completionFull

K-7 is the most reliably productive subject in the study's history, and the only participant whose partial match rate (16.1% of sessions) meaningfully exceeds the team's estimated baseline. Robinson described her in his field notes as "the most remarkable participant we've encountered" — a characterisation the team considers warranted by the data, though it is noted that K-7 was also one of the study's most engaged and analytically minded participants, which introduces confounds that cannot be entirely ruled out.

K-7's musical background may be relevant. In several sessions she described dream content in musical terms — sequences, intervals, positions within a structure — and on two occasions identified card suits through what she described as "tonal associations" (she reported that the Diamond suit "feels like a high register, clear and precise," while the Club suit "feels low and resonant, like a cello in a small room"). Neither of these associations was prompted by the team; both appeared spontaneously in her journals.

The most significant session — the first confirmed partial success of the study — is documented in Field Note Entry #078. K-7 was informed of the correspondence at the end of that session's debrief. Her response was to ask, very precisely, how many sessions had produced similar results across all subjects. When given the figure, she said: "So it's rare. But it happens." She did not express surprise.

Dream journal extract — Session 19, 9 September 1969 (see Field Note Entry #078)
"I was in a market, but the goods weren't named — they were numbered. Long rows of them, each with a small card attached that said only a number. I was looking for one in particular. I knew its number the way you know a telephone number you've dialled many times: not by reciting it but by feel. I knew it was the thirty-fourth one. I walked along the row until I reached it. The item there was small and slightly warm in my hand. I didn't look at it directly."

K-7 maintained correspondence with Robinson after the study closed, as noted in Field Note Entry #097. She is the only subject known to have continued experiencing study-related phenomena (self-reported) after the completion of her participation.

Study completionWithdrew — Session 12

M-11 withdrew from the study in February 1967, citing what he described as "persistent intrusive imagery" — specifically, a recurring visual motif he connected to one of the stimulus images, which he reported continuing to see involuntarily during waking hours in the weeks following Session 7 (the mirror and spiral session; see Field Note Entry #034). The ethics panel reviewed the withdrawal and concluded that no formal adverse event had occurred, but the incident is noted here for completeness.

M-11 was nonetheless among the more interesting subjects in his cohort during his active participation. His journals were sparse by comparison with A-3 or K-7 — typically less than a page — but contained unusually precise observations. In Session 9, he reported: "a number, forty-one, presented to me by a figure I understood to be a librarian, though I couldn't see their face." The card assigned that evening occupied position 39 in the master ordering. The team scored this as a near match; M-11 was not informed of the correspondence before his withdrawal.

Dream journal extract — Session 7, 29 October 1966
"I was standing behind my own reflection. Not in front of a mirror — behind it, looking out at myself looking in. The other me was older. It was smiling at something I couldn't see. I didn't like it. I woke up and lay still for a while before I was able to write this down."

Age (at enrolment)22
OccupationUniversity student (biology)
Total sessions8
Outcome — Full match0
Outcome — Partial match0

Study completionFull (8 sessions scheduled)

P-2 produced no matches of any category across her eight sessions, making her the study's clearest null case in the first cohort. However, Robinson noted in his field records that her dream journals were, in his words, "the most vividly written in the study" — rich in sensory detail and unusual in their emotional precision, though consistently free of numeric content and bearing no apparent relation to the assigned stimulus images.

The team considers P-2 an important data point: she demonstrates that vivid and elaborate dream recall is not, in itself, a predictor of symbolic encoding. Her engagement with the imagery during induction sessions was evident and genuine; she described the images as "genuinely beautiful" and found the study premise fascinating. The absence of any correspondence suggests that the mechanism, if real, does not operate uniformly across individuals regardless of their receptiveness to the imagery.

P-2 was not available for the second cohort due to academic commitments. The team noted her absence as a loss. She was fully debriefed at the end of her participation; her only comment on the null results was that she found it "oddly reassuring" that her dreams remained her own.

Dream journal extract — Session 4, 15 October 1966
"I was standing in a field at night. The grass was silver and made a sound when I walked through it, like paper. There was no moon but there was plenty of light, coming from no direction in particular. Far away, something was moving — something large, unhurried, not threatening. I watched it for a long time. When I woke up I felt as though I had been somewhere very real and had been asked to leave."

Study completionWithdrew — health (non-adverse)

R-6 withdrew after seven sessions due to a health matter unrelated to the study — a cardiac episode in December 1966 that required a period of recuperation at home. The ethics panel was notified; the medical team confirmed no causal connection to participation. R-6 expressed his regret at being unable to continue and told Robinson he had found the sessions "more interesting than I expected, which is saying something at my age."

The most notable moment in R-6's participation came in Session 3, which the team did not score formally as a match but which was discussed at length in the subsequent review meeting. R-6 reported in his journal that he had dreamed of a map in which one location was circled, "and I knew with complete certainty that it was the seventeenth location on the map, counting from the top-left corner." The card assigned that evening was in position 17. He was not informed of the correspondence before his withdrawal, and the team did not contact him afterward — a decision Robinson later described, in his 1972 addendum, as "one I'm less comfortable with in retrospect."

Dream journal extract — Session 3, 14 October 1966
"A map, hand-drawn, on thick paper. There were many locations marked — I couldn't read the names, but I could count the marks. One was circled in red ink. I knew it was the seventeenth. Not because I counted to it — I just knew, the way you know which key is for which door before you've reached the lock. I woke up thinking about maps I'd drawn thirty years ago."
